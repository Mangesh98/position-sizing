Position Sizing in Trading

What Is Position Sizing?

An investor or trader's position size refers to the number of units they have invested in a particular security. It is important to take into account an investor's account size and risk tolerance when determining the size of a position.

Understanding Position Sizing

Position sizing refers to the size of a position within a portfolio or the amount an investor will invest. In order to maximize returns and control risk, investors use position sizing to determine how many units of securities they can purchase. Although position sizing applies to most investment types, it is most closely associated with day trading and currency trading

Despite its importance in nearly all investment types, position sizing is most closely associated with day trading and currency trading (forex).

KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Position sizing refers to how many units an investor or trader invests in a particular security.
  • An investor's risk tolerance and account size must be considered when determining appropriate position sizing.
  • The concept of position sizing is important in most types of investments, but it is most closely associated with faster-moving investors like currency traders and day traders.
  • In spite of proper position sizing, investors may lose more than their specified risk limits if a stock gaps below their stop-loss order.

Position Sizing Example

In order to determine the best position sizing, we must weigh three different factors:

Account Risk

Investors must determine their account risk before using appropriate position sizing for a specific trade. Typically, this is expressed as a percentage of the investor's capital. In general, retail investors should not risk more than 2% of their investment capital on any one trade; fund managers usually risk less.

If an investor has a $25,000 account and chooses to set their maximum account risk at 2%, they cannot risk more than $500 per trade (2% x $25,000). Despite losing 10 consecutive trades, the investor has only lost 20% of their investment.

Trade Risk

The investor must then decide where to place their stop-loss order. In stock trading, the trade risk is the difference between the intended entry price and the stop-loss price, expressed in dollars. As an example, if an investor intends to purchase Apple Inc. at $160 and places a stop-loss order at $140, the trade risk is $20 per share.

Proper Position Size

The investor now knows that they can risk $500 per trade and are risking $20 per share. The investor can calculate the correct position size by dividing the account risk, which is $500, by the trade risk, which is $20. In this case, 25 shares can be purchased ($500 / $20).

Position Sizing and Gap Risk

In spite of correct position sizing, investors may lose more than their specified account risk limit if a stock gaps below their stop-loss order. To reduce gap risk, investors may want to halve their position size before company earnings announcements.
